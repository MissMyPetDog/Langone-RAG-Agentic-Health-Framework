### Answer (generated by LLM based on literature)

The patient exhibits a significant rise in serum creatinine from 1.00 to 2.20 mg/dL within 15.6 hours, indicating acute kidney injury (AKI). She has a history of myocardial infarction, congestive heart failure, COPD, mild liver disease, and chronic kidney disease, with the use of nephrotoxic drugs. Managing her AKI involves addressing perfusion, reducing nephrotoxic exposure, and supporting renal function, considering her complex medical history.

1. **Discontinue Nephrotoxic Agents:**
   - **Why this patient:** The creatinine increase to 2.20 mg/dL suggests nephrotoxic-induced AKI could be worsening her condition. Discontinuing nephrotoxic agents is critical [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Avoid if the drugs are essential and no alternatives exist. It requires careful balance, especially with concurrent infections necessitating specific classes of antibiotics.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].

2. **Ensure Volume Status and Perfusion Pressure:**
   - **Why this patient:** The significant and rapid rise in creatinine indicates possible hypoperfusion. Correcting volume status can improve renal perfusion, especially in heart failure cases [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Must be cautious in congestive heart failure patients to avoid volume overload. Hemodynamic monitoring may be appropriate.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].

3. **Functional Hemodynamic Monitoring:**
   - **Why this patient:** Given the patient's age and the cardiovascular history including congestive heart failure, functional hemodynamic monitoring can guide fluid therapy to avoid volume overload while maintaining adequate perfusion [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** While useful, invasive monitoring may be complex and should be balanced against risks of procedures in a patient with COPD and other chronic conditions.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p12)].

Fallback logic: If discontinuation of nephrotoxins is not feasible (e.g., critical infection management), emphasize volume status and perfusion through monitoring as alternative management approaches.
