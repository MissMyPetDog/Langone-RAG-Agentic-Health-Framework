The pharmacologic treatment for dementia, specifically dementia with Lewy bodies, includes the use of acetylcholinesterase inhibitors and other agents. 

Rivastigmine, an acetylcholinesterase inhibitor, has shown effectiveness in reducing neuropsychiatric symptoms such as delusions, visual hallucinations, apathy, and depression in patients with dementia with Lewy bodies compared to placebo [pmid_26595642_p32]. Donepezil has also been noted to improve such symptoms, although specific recruitment of patients with troublesome visual hallucinations was not conducted [pmid_26595642_p32].

Quetiapine, although often used by clinicians to treat psychotic symptoms in dementia with Lewy bodies, had limited evidence supporting its efficacy, as a placebo-controlled study reported no effect on agitation and psychosis compared to placebo [pmid_26595642_p31].

Clozapine has been used to reduce visual hallucinations in Parkinson’s disease and dementia; however, severe dementia was often an exclusion criterion in most studies, limiting conclusive evidence for its use in dementia [pmid_26595642_p31].

Additionally, memantine or acetylcholinesterase inhibitors may improve REM sleep behavior disorder (RBD) in some cases associated with Lewy body dementias [pmid_26595642_p33].

Current evidence indicates limited pharmacologic treatments with established efficacy for dementia, emphasizing the need for continued research and consultation with healthcare providers for personalized care.
