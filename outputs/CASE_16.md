Managing acute kidney injury (AKI) in a 64-year-old male patient with multiple comorbidities, including myocardial infarction, congestive heart failure, and peripheral vascular disease, while considering his current medication regimen of ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and diuretics, requires careful selection of therapeutic strategies. These choices must balance the potential benefits and risks of interventions, particularly with the possibility of drug interactions and exacerbation of existing conditions.

1. **Avoidance of Tight Volume Management**: In patients with AKI, especially those with congestive heart failure (CHF), careful volume management is essential. While diuretics can help manage fluid overload, their use should be cautious due to the risk of worsening renal function. The patient is already on diuretics, and further aggressive diuretic use could potentially exacerbate AKI or CHF. Ensuring euvolemia without causing dehydration is recommended. The KDIGO guidelines suggest avoiding overly aggressive diuresis as a treatment for AKI [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p56)].

2. **Stage-Appropriate Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)**: While the patient’s current creatinine level indicates mild AKI, close monitoring is recommended to assess the progression of renal impairment. Initiating RRT is typically reserved for severe cases. In any progressive or severe AKI scenario, considering early consultation with nephrology for potential RRT could become relevant, ensuring it’s appropriate for the patient's cardiac status [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p2)].

3. **Discontinuation or Adjustment of Renin-Angiotensin System Inhibitors (RASIs)**: The continuation of ACE inhibitors and ARBs in the setting of AKI can be controversial. These medications can reduce intraglomerular pressure and potentially worsen renal function in AKI. Therefore, temporary discontinuation or dose reduction should be considered, especially given the patient's history of myocardial infarction and peripheral vascular disease where optimal management of these drugs is crucial. This decision should involve careful monitoring of renal parameters and hemodynamic status [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p56)].

Fallback logic involves careful monitoring and adjustment of the therapy plan in response to the patient’s renal function and overall clinical status. If a diuretic regimen is ineffective or unsafe due to hypotension or worsening renal function, further cardiac assessment and potential RRT might be warranted. Similarly, if RASIs exacerbation of AKI is evident, adjusting the medication regimen should be considered as a secondary step to managing AKI.
