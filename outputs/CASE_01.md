### Answer (generated by LLM based on literature)

The patient presents with acute kidney injury (AKI) alongside severe liver disease, hypertension, malignancy, and coagulopathy, while also being on a diuretic and antibiotic. Given this complexity, managing AKI with careful consideration to potential nephrotoxicity and drug-drug interactions, especially in the context of severe liver disease, is essential. Her condition is compounded by potentially reduced renal perfusion and fluid management challenges, commonly seen in this multifaceted clinical scenario.

1. **Furosemide for Volume Overload:**
   - **Why this patient:** Given the AKI context with likely fluid overload due to AKI and possible ascites from liver disease, using furosemide to manage volume overload could be beneficial. However, its use should be under restricted circumstances and closely monitored as furosemide is not effective in minimizing mortality or RRT requirements in AKI. The presence of complications such as elevated serum creatinine would require careful cardiac and circulatory status assessment before initiation [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p50].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Primarily used when symptoms indicate volume overload. Avoid in hypovolemia or renal hypoperfusion, as excessive use could exacerbate the injury by further decreasing renal perfusion [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p50].
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p50], [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p10].

2. **Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT):**
   - **Why this patient:** In the presence of severe AKI, especially if the patient shows signs of significant oliguria or persistent electrolyte imbalances that cannot be controlled medically, RRT may be indicated to replace renal function and stabilize the patient’s fluid and electrolyte status [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p10].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Indicated in cases of severe and intractable fluid overload, hyperkalemia, or acidosis unresponsive to medical treatment. Contraindications include temporary improvements in renal function or if risks of invasive procedures outweigh benefits [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p8].
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p10], [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p8].

3. **Albumin Infusion for Support:**
   - **Why this patient:** In severe liver disease with hypoalbuminemia, albumin infusion can assist in managing circulatory dysfunction and augment albumin levels, thereby potentially stabilizing hemodynamic status[Intraarterial ICU Intensive-care unit, pmid_23499048_p10]. This supports intravascular volume expansion without the nephrotoxic risks associated with certain diuretics.
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Useful in patients with hypoalbuminemia and ascites or those at risk for hepatorenal syndrome. Avoid or use cautiously in patients where volume overload without adequate hemodynamic monitoring might be detrimental [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p8].
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary, pmid_23499048_p10], [Intraarterial ICU Intensive-care unit, pmid_23499048_p10].

These options must be weighed against the patient’s liver function, current hemodynamic status, and the presence of contraindicated conditions. If a patient-specific contraindication arises regarding the use of furosemide (e.g., worsening renal function or prerenal azotemia), RRT should be considered as the next intervention. Should both pharmacological and basic RRT options be unsuitable, consider volume and hemodynamic support through albumin infusions. Consultation with nephrology and hepatology specialists is recommended for clinical decision-making.

Chapter 3.4: The use of diuretics in AKI Diuretics are frequently used in patients at risk of AKI, and in the management of those who develop AKI. Since ﬂuid overload is one of the major symptoms of AKI, diuretics are often used for patients with AKI to facilitate ﬂuid manage- ment. Recent observational studies showed that 59–70% of patients with AKI were given diuretics at the time of nephrology consultation or before the start of RRT.181,182 In addition, oliguric AKI has a worse prognosis than nonoliguric AKI and physicians often prescribe diuretics to convert oliguric to nonoliguric AKI.183 Diuretics are also used to control ﬂuid balance and permit administration of nutrition and medications. Furthermore, several diuretics have potentially renoprotective effects that might prevent development of AKI and hasten its recovery. However, diuretics can also be harmful, by reducing the circulating volume excessively and adding a prerenal insult, worsening established AKI. Therefore, it is essential to evaluate usefulness of diuretics to improve outcome of patients with AKI, not just for ﬂuid management. 3.4.1: We recommend not using diuretics to prevent AKI. Loop diuretics act at the luminal surface of the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle and inhibit the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter,184,185 resulting in a loss of the high medullary osmolality and decreased ability to reabsorb water. Inhibition of active sodium transport also reduces renal tubular oxygen consumption, potentially decreasing ischemic damage of the most vulnerable outer medullary tubular segments;183 therefore, furosemide might protect kidneys against ischemic injury.186 Furosemide also might hasten recovery of AKI by washing out necrotic debris blocking tubules, and by inhibiting prostaglandin dehydro- genase, which reduces renovascular resistance and increases renal blood ﬂow.186,187 Based on these properties, loop diuretics might be expected to prevent or ameliorate AKI.
