The patient's acute kidney injury (AKI) and fluid imbalance in the context of significant comorbidities such as chronic kidney disease, myocardial infarction, diabetes, and sepsis, present a complex clinical challenge. Key clinical parameters include a history of nephrotoxic agent use, hypertension, and moderate/severe renal disease in addition to the contraindication for ACEI/ARB and NSAIDs. The optimization of renal function and fluid balance requires careful management given these constraints.

1. **Intravenous Fluid Administration (preferably isotonic saline):**
   - **Why this patient:** The patient exhibits fluid imbalance due to sepsis and AKI, where guided fluid resuscitation using isotonic saline can be used to manage kidney perfusion and address hypovolemia caused by septic shock, thus potentially improving renal function [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p3); (pmid_23499048_p76)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** This strategy is favorable when volume expansion is safe and effective, but it requires monitoring to avoid fluid overload, particularly in patients with CHF. Avoided if there is significant pulmonary congestion.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p76); (pmid_23499048_p8)].

2. **N-acetylcysteine (NAC):**
   - **Why this patient:** Given the patient's history of exposure to nephrotoxic agents, NAC can provide renal protective effects against oxidant injury in AKI, especially relevant with recent exposure to nephrotoxic antibiotics [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p3)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Appropriate when the risk of contrast-induced AKI exists or nephrotoxicity is a concern; contraindicated if hypersensitivity or adverse reaction to NAC is known.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p10)].

3. **Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT):**
   - **Why this patient:** The presence of multiple risks including severe renal dysfunction and reduced urine output (not quantified in the context, but typically inferred in severe AKI and CKD cases) supports consideration for RRT as a means to manage solute removal and fluid balance in the context of CKD and severe AKI [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p8)].
   - **When to use / contraindications:** Best used in cases of volume overload refractory to diuretics, severe electrolyte imbalances, or ongoing signs of uremia; consider potential complications like infection or hemodynamic instability during initiation.
   - **Supporting sources:** [KDOQI US commentary on the 2012 KDIGO clinical practice guideline for acute kidney injury (pmid_23499048_p8)].

Fallback consideration: If IV fluid administration is contraindicated due to CHF (e.g., pulmonary congestion), prioritize the use of NAC or initiate RRT as guided by fluid overload or resistant uremia.
